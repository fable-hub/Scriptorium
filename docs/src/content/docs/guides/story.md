:::note
This is a imaginary story, trying to explain the allegory behind the Scriptorium family of libraries.

It is not meant to be taken literally, but rather to illustrate the design principles and relationships between the libraries in a memorable way.
:::

> *Scriptorium: a room in a monastery where manuscripts were copied and illuminated by hand.*

The scribe sits down and fills the pot of **Ink** - colour and style, ready to give life to every stroke. <span style="text-decoration:underline">Bold or dim, red or green</span>, the Ink asks no questions. It simply waits to be used.

The scribe reaches for a sheet of **Parchment** and begins to write. The words appear structured, purposeful - <span style="text-decoration:underline">a log of what happened, what was warned, what went wrong</span>. Parchment dips into the Ink pot to illuminate its headings: a cyan `[INFO]`, a yellow `[WARN]`, a red `[ERROR]`. The record is clear. The page holds everything.

Then the scribe takes the Quill - and it is the **Nib** at its tip that does the real work. That sharp, precise point is where *expectation meets reality*. The Nib touches the Parchment and either the mark is true, or it is not. Every flaw is recorded without interruption; <span style="text-decoration:underline">the hand does not stop mid-stroke</span>. Only when the page is full does the Quill lift - and every deviation is reported at once.

The **Quill** itself is the instrument that holds all of this together. It carries the Nib, dips into the Ink, and lays the results onto the Parchment - a complete manuscript of your test run, illuminated in *green* for what passed, *red* for what failed, and *dim grey* for what was left unwritten.
